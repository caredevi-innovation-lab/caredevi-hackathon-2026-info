HEAT EMERGENCY TRIAGE PROTOCOL

HEAT EXHAUSTION — ESI 3
Definition: heat-related illness with core temperature <40°C (104°F), with intact CNS function.
Symptoms: heavy sweating, cool/moist skin, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, muscle cramps, tachycardia, hypotension.
Treatment: move to cool environment, oral hydration if alert, IV NS 1-2L if unable to drink, cooling measures (fans, ice packs to neck/axilla/groin), rest.
Disposition: most can be discharged after recovery, with instructions to avoid heat for 24-48h.

HEAT STROKE — ESI 1
Definition: core temp >40°C (104°F) with CNS dysfunction (confusion, ataxia, bizarre behavior, seizure, coma).
Two types:
- Classic: elderly, chronic illness, medications, poor heat dissipation. Often dry skin (anhidrosis).
- Exertional: young athletes/military, intense exercise in heat. Often still sweating.

IMMEDIATE COOLING IS THE TREATMENT — every minute matters:
Target core temp: <38.9°C (102°F) within 30 minutes.
Fastest method: cold water immersion (ice bath) — gold standard for exertional. Cold water at 1-15°C.
Alternatives: evaporative cooling (mist fans + ice packs), cooling blankets, cold IV fluids.
Avoid: antipyretics (NSAIDs/acetaminophen) — ineffective and potentially harmful.
Ice packs to: neck, axillae, groin (major vessels).

MEDICATION CONSIDERATIONS — CRITICAL:
Beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol): IMPAIR thermoregulation — blunt tachycardia response, reduce sweating. Patients on beta-blockers may not mount appropriate tachycardia — HR may appear misleadingly normal despite severe heat stress.
Diuretics (furosemide, HCTZ): cause dehydration + electrolyte loss — exacerbate heat illness.
Anticholinergics (diphenhydramine, TCAs, antipsychotics): impair sweating.
Stimulants (amphetamines, cocaine): increase heat production.
Alcohol: causes vasodilation + diuresis.

RISK FACTORS:
Age extremes: infants, elderly. Obesity. Cardiac disease. Renal disease. Prior heat stroke.
Environmental: high humidity + high temperature (wet bulb temperature >32°C most dangerous), no air conditioning, outdoor exertion in heat.
Medications: as listed above.

COMPLICATIONS:
Rhabdomyolysis: CK >1000 U/L, myoglobinuria — aggressive IV hydration, target UO >200 mL/hr.
Acute kidney injury: aggressive hydration.
Coagulopathy/DIC: monitor coagulation, fresh frozen plasma if needed.
Hepatic injury: elevated LFTs common.
CNS: cerebral edema, seizures — benzos for seizure control.

ELDERLY-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS:
Core temperature >38°C in elderly patient with confusion: treat as heat emergency even without classic criteria.
Check: medication list for beta-blockers, diuretics, anticholinergics.
Social history: air conditioning access, living alone, dementia.
